Stacks · Co-founder & sole builder · 2026 Live with real users

A WhatsApp AI nutritionist, built end to end by one person

The idea

Most supplement advice is generic. Stacks pairs a real conversation with real personalisation: users chat to Molly, an AI nutritionist on WhatsApp, about their goals, diet, lifestyle and blood work, and get a supplement stack recommended for them specifically.

What I built

Stacks is a two-person venture, and the entire build is mine. The product, the conversational design, the AI logic and safety behaviour, the website at stackssupplements.ai, and the operations behind it: users can send text, voice notes or photos; conversations are stored and reviewed through an internal dashboard; and an automated evaluator scores every conversation so quality problems surface before users complain.

Concept to live bot in two weeks, now in production with real users. I am a non-coder who directs AI tooling to build; this is the proof that works.

Calo Inc. · Principal Product Manager · 2024 to 2025 Beta at departure

An AI health coach that closes the gap between what people log and what they eat

The problem

Calo delivers personalised meals to hundreds of thousands of clients across the GCC and UK. The number one reason people cancelled: "I'm not seeing results." The cause usually wasn't the meals, it was everything else: snacks, weekends, exercise, the untracked calories nobody logs.

What I did

Led an AI health coach from concept to live internal testing. It lives on WhatsApp, so there is no new app to adopt. It connects Apple Health data and a custom link into Calo's backend. I worked with nutritionists to define clinical guardrails, disclaimers and escalation paths, partnered with engineering on the architecture, and tuned the AI's prompts myself.

By using workflow tooling and third-party services first, we had a working beta in weeks rather than months, deliberately trading polish for speed to learning.

Independent AI consulting · Sole consultant · 2026, ongoing Live in pilot

An AI quoting agent for a family-run manufacturer, sold and delivered solo

The engagement

A family-run UK custom clothing manufacturer (around 500 orders a month) was drowning in manual work: quotes written from memory, follow-ups missed, systems that didn't talk to each other. I won the work via referral and ran the whole engagement myself: scoped it, priced it, sold it, delivered it.

The diagnosis

The real constraint was team capacity against growing demand, not missing software. Previous automation attempts had failed because they removed the human touch the business is known for. So every solution keeps a person in control: the AI drafts, a rep reviews and presses send.

What shipped

An AI quoting agent that reads new enquiries, prices them from a governed price list I established as the single source of truth (fixing years of inconsistent pricing along the way), and drafts an on-brand quote straight into the rep's outbox. Around it: automated day-2 and day-7 follow-ups, and automatic pipeline hygiene in the CRM, so the sales data stays honest with zero extra effort from reps.

Fresh Fitness Food + Detox Kitchen · Chief Product Officer · 2019 to 2024 Acquired by Calo

From Google Sheets to a platform that carried the business to £10m and an acquisition

The starting point

Fresh Fitness Food delivered personalised meals across London, and ran almost entirely on spreadsheets and manual workflows. I was the only product hire, promoted from within after making the case that I understood the customer deeply enough to design the platform.

What I built

The entire digital ecosystem from zero: supplier ordering, client management, subscription billing, kitchen operations, delivery logistics. I started by mapping every manual workflow in the business and interviewing every team, then sequenced the build by commercial impact: supplier ordering first (the biggest inefficiency), customer ordering second (the biggest revenue lever), then kitchen and logistics.

When FFF acquired Detox Kitchen, the platform absorbed a second brand: separate customer-facing experiences, one merged operational back end. When Calo acquired FFF, the nationwide footprint that platform enabled was a material part of the acquisition story, and I then led the migration of both brands onto Calo's platform, keeping 95% of active customers.

Four decisions, honestly told

Anyone can list wins. These are the judgement calls that shaped the results above, including the one I lost.

Kill your own strategy

The app I launched as an acquisition tool, then gutted on purpose

I built FFF's iOS app on a freemium acquisition hypothesis: free wellness features converting users to paid meal plans. Post-launch cohort analysis showed free-to-paid conversion under 1%, and the wellness features barely used. But app-active paying clients had ~30% lower churn at 60 days. So I killed the wellness layer and redirected all engineering to service features: skip a delivery, change nutrition, one-tap reorder.

Retention in subscription doesn't come from engagement features. It comes from removing friction in the core job customers hired you for.

Resist the obvious merge

Two brands, one engine

When FFF acquired Detox Kitchen, the obvious play was a full merge. I made a different call: keep the front ends completely separate (different sites, brands, audiences) and amalgamate operations behind the scenes: one kitchen, one logistics network, one platform. Both brands kept their identity; the business kept the efficiency.

The instinct to merge brands is strong in acquisitions, and it usually destroys value.

The one I lost

I argued against a payments migration, got overruled, and learned why I lost

I pushed back on migrating billing from Stripe to Checkout.com. I was overruled, and the migration ran 2.5x over budget with edge cases surfacing for six months. Two years later we migrated back. I don't relish being right: it cost the business real money, and I own my part in failing to stop it.

When a CEO makes a commercial argument, counter with a better commercial argument, not an engineering one.

Luck is not a strategy

The expansion bet that got bailed out

I designed FFF's nationwide expansion: all UK postcodes at once, tiered delivery pricing as a self-selecting filter. Revenue underperformed the forecast; premium pricing didn't travel. But the nationwide footprint became a material part of why Calo acquired the business. The bet was rescued by a variable I had underweighted.

That's lucky, not designed. I now weight strategic optionality explicitly in every decision model, not just the primary commercial case.

About

I moved from clinical nutrition into product for one reason: a nutritionist helps one person at a time; a product helps everyone who uses it. That decision has shaped every role since.

The science background isn't a nice-to-have, it's a working tool. I hold a BSc in Human Nutrition and Physiology, which means I can sit between clinicians, engineers and commercial teams and translate all three. In health tech, where getting the clinical detail wrong breaks user trust (or worse), that combination is rare in a PM.

I operate best with high autonomy and ambiguity: sole product functions, unplanned acquisitions, platforms built for one market being adapted for another. Give me clarity on the priority and the constraint, and I run with it.

"AI products don't fail because the model isn't good enough. They fail because they don't fit the workflow, there's no clinical rigour, or nobody defined success before shipping."
